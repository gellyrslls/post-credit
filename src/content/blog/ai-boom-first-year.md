I went to university and enrolled in Computer Science in 2022. That December, OpenAI released ChatGPT. I remember seeing it discussed online in the way that things get discussed when people are not sure yet whether something is a novelty or a turning point. Within a few weeks it was clear it was not a novelty. By the time I started my second semester, the conversation about what a CS education was supposed to be preparing you for had already begun to shift — quietly at first, then loudly and all at once.

## A Curriculum in the Middle of Changing

There is a strange experience that happens when the industry your program is training you for reorganizes itself mid-program. Some of what I was taught became more valuable — fundamentals, systems thinking, the ability to reason about problems abstractly. Some of it became less immediately relevant — specific tools, specific stacks, categories of work that are now being partially automated. The curriculum did not fully adapt in real time, because curricula cannot. It was designed for a job market that existed before I enrolled, updated at the pace of academic review cycles, while the actual job market was changing at the pace of product launches.

## The Pressure of Self-Teaching

What this created, practically, was a second track that ran alongside the formal program. I was learning what school offered in class, and I was learning what the industry was actually asking for on my own time. Prompt engineering, AI-assisted development workflows, new tooling, shifting expectations around what a junior developer was expected to know before day one — none of that was in the syllabus, and all of it affected how employers were evaluating the profiles of new graduates. You could ignore the second track, but ignoring it had a cost that would show up later.

## Graduating Into It

I am finishing my degree in a job market where the tools I will use daily did not exist when I enrolled, where the role of junior developer is actively being redefined, and where every hiring conversation includes an implicit or explicit question about how you work with AI. I do not say this with alarm — I say it because it is the honest description of the situation, and honest descriptions are more useful than either panic or reassurance. The degree is real. The foundation is real. What I am walking into is also real, and unfamiliar in ways that no batch before mine had to navigate quite like this. That is not a disadvantage. It is just the context I am starting from.
