I enrolled in Computer Science at USC in August 2022. That December, OpenAI released ChatGPT. I know exactly when I found out about it because the timing was too specific to forget, which was about a week or two before my final exams, first semester, still fully online. I was staring at C programming assessments involving nested loops that I understood conceptually but had no real practice with, and I was completely out of time. ChatGPT did not feel like a technological turning point in that moment. It felt like an intervention. It felt like something that had arrived specifically to save my semester, and I let it.

That is the honest version of how the AI era started for me.

## The First Year Nobody Talks About

My first semester of university was still online. Which meant it was also, in practice, a continuation of the two years of online classes I had just come from during the pandemic. The new habits I was supposed to build, the new environment I was supposed to adapt to, the fresh start that moving from Saudi Arabia to the Philippines was supposed to represent; none of that materialized the way I imagined because I was still sitting in the same room, staring at the same screen, falling into the same unproductive patterns I had spent a year and a half developing.

When I found ChatGPT the week before finals, I knew it felt wrong. I want to be clear about that. I understood the nested loop problems well enough to follow the logic when I read through them. What I did not have was the time or the motivation to sit down and actually practice them from scratch, because I had spent the semester not building that habit. ChatGPT filled the gap I had created for myself. I used it for the hard parts of the assessments and I passed. The guilt was real and quiet and I carried it into second semester.

Second semester was face-to-face. And that changed everything in a way I was not prepared for. The first time stepping onto the USC campus. The first time sitting in a classroom after years of just being inside my house. The first time commuting, the first time meeting my batchmates in person, the first time realizing that some of the people I was sitting next to had already been building things for years. The imposter syndrome that arrived in that semester was different from the kind I had been carrying online. Online, it was easy to hide. Face-to-face, there was nowhere to put it. I was confident in my skills in a general sense, but I was also aware, very suddenly, that a lot of what I had done in first semester was putting up a front. The habits had not actually changed. The guilt had just moved locations.

## Watching the Curriculum Fall Behind in Real Time

By second year, the conversation around AI had shifted from novelty to noise. Every week there was a new model, a new tool, a new capability that made something that had seemed difficult last month feel suddenly accessible. I was watching this happen while sitting in classes that were, in some cases, teaching me things that felt like they were designed for a different version of the industry than the one that was clearly forming outside.

This is not entirely the university's fault. Curriculum design moves on a cycle that does not respond to product launches. No institution can redesign its program every six months because the industry reorganized itself again. But the practical effect, sitting inside the program while it was happening, was that there was the official learning track and then there was the real one where you were teaching yourself what the job market was actually starting to ask for, on your own time, without a syllabus.

The honest thing about how AI use evolved for me across four years is that it changed shape completely. First year was using it to survive assessments I had not prepared for. By third and fourth year, working on TC Insights and going through my internship at Symph, it became something different where a tool I was using alongside actual domain knowledge, to go faster on things I already understood, to unstick problems I had genuinely engaged with. That shift matters. The difference between using AI as a replacement for learning and using it as an extension of existing knowledge is the difference between building a dependency and building a workflow. It took me most of my degree to learn the distinction by getting it wrong first.

## The "What's The Point" Feeling

There is a specific kind of demoralization that hits when you realize that a skill you spent years building can now be approximated, to a meaningful degree, by someone with no background describing what they want in plain English. I felt that. Most people I know in tech felt that. It is the ambient undercurrent of almost every conversation about the industry right now, whether people name it directly or not.

The feeling has shifted for me, though not because it became less true. It became less central. What I kept coming back to was that the tools do not replace having something real to show. They narrow the gap for certain tasks, but they do not produce the thing you built, the problem you identified, the users who actually depend on what you made. TC Insights exists because I understood the specific inefficiency in our editorial workflow and had the technical fluency to build a solution for it. An AI tool did not identify that problem. I did. The value of what I know is not just in the code I can write, but also in the judgment about what is worth building and why.

That framing did not come from optimism. It came from realizing that what the shrinking gap actually means is that the bar shifted. It used to be enough to know the syntax. Now the expectation is that you know the system, specifically, how the pieces fit together at scale, how architecture decisions create constraints downstream, how to make something that survives production and real users and changing requirements. Those things are harder to shortcut. They also happen to be the things I feel least prepared for, which is its own uncomfortable truth to sit with six weeks from graduation.

## Graduating Into the Thing That Was There From The Start

There is a phrase I keep encountering when I read about this industry moment: unconscious incompetence. Not knowing what you do not know. The particular challenge of graduating right now is that the field of unknown unknowns is unusually large, because a significant portion of what the job market expects was invented or renamed in the last two years. MLOps. Agentic systems. RAG pipelines. LLM integration. These are not things that appeared on a syllabus I missed. They are disciplines that materialized mid-degree while I was studying for courses designed before they existed.

The response to that is not panic. It is also not pretending the gap is smaller than it is. Software engineering has always been a field where the moment you stop learning is the moment you actually fall behind. AI did not create that condition, it just accelerated it and made the stakes more visible. What I am graduating with is a foundation, a real project that real people use, an internship that showed me what production software actually looks like, and a very clear map of where my edges are. Filling those edges in is just the next part of the work.

I enrolled when the AI era started. I used the tools badly before I used them well. I watched a curriculum struggle to keep up with an industry that was not waiting for it. Now I am graduating into a job market built on the same technology I was using to cut corners in my first semester, which is either deeply ironic or just what happens when you stay in a field long enough to meet the consequences of your own starting point.

Either way, I am still here. That counts for something.
