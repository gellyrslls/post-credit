There is a strange experience that happens when the industry your program is training you for reorganizes itself mid-program. When I enrolled in Computer Science in August 2022, the roadmap felt predictable. By December of that same year, the entire foundation of the industry began to shift quietly at first, and then loudly and all at once.

## The Discord Server and the Star Pyramid

When December 2022 arrived, I was at home trying to find a way to pass my Programming 1 finals. Because we were still coming out of the pandemic, the Department of Computer Science at USC was conducting classes entirely online. My first real encounter with generative AI happened while I was stuck on a classic freshman assignment. I vividly remember joining an OpenAI Discord server just to get early access to the GPT-3.5 series. 

Our programming language was C, and all of our assessments were done on an automated platform called CodeChum. I was staring at a problem requiring me to build a full star pyramid, which meant I needed to figure out the exact logic for an outer loop for the rows and two inner loops for the leading spaces and stars. It was exactly the kind of fundamental problem I was struggling with. Out of curiosity, I pasted the prompt into the chat, and the model instantly generated C code that passed every single test case. As a beginner, I was completely amazed, and I immediately began relying on it for similar problems. It felt like a cheat code, and I did not fully grasp the implications of what I had just found.

## The Open Secret

Our first face-to-face classes finally started in January 2023, which was the second semester of my first year. By then, the popularity of ChatGPT had skyrocketed. It quickly became an open secret across the campus.

The strange part was sitting in physical classrooms and watching the disconnect happen in real time. People were not just using it for code anymore. They were generating every single writing assignment and essay needed for our general electives, including our ethics classes. The curriculum was moving at its normal, deliberate pace, but the reality of how students were completing the work had fundamentally changed. We were essentially living in two different timelines, learning what school offered on the board while quietly surviving the requirements using a browser tab.

## From Cheating Syntax to Building Systems

The novelty of having a chatbot write basic C code eventually wore off, and the actual groundbreaking shift happened when I started using tools like Claude and Gemini to build real software. 

In late 2024, I had to build MediVault, which is a comprehensive pharmacy inventory management system. I needed to engineer a secure Node.js backend with JWT authentication and optimize database queries using Prisma and PostgreSQL. Rather than writing every line manually, I gave Claude MCP the proper context and the exact project requirements from my professor. I focused entirely on trying to meet the minimum viable product, and it worked, even though I did not understand every single piece of the underlying architecture at the time. 

This acceleration became even more apparent when I developed TC Insights for *Today's Carolinian*. I engineered an internal analytics platform using Next.js and Supabase that integrates the Meta Graph API to automatically fetch data and rank content through a custom percentile-based algorithm. Over twenty editorial managers actively use this dashboard today. Building a robust, secure tool of this scale as a solo student developer would normally take an entire year, but AI tools allowed me to ship it in a fraction of the time.

## The Trade-Off of Moving Faster

I am very aware of what I missed out on learning because of this reliance. I did not spend endless hours memorizing framework documentation or manually debugging routing issues the way previous generations of computer science students had to do. I skipped a lot of the traditional struggle that builds rote muscle memory.

However, my perspective is that the trade-off is absolutely worth it. Abstracting away the boilerplate code allowed me to focus entirely on product logic, user experience, and system architecture. Experiencing what it feels like to build a project that is genuinely important and usable by real people taught me more about professional software engineering than writing manual CRUD operations ever could. AI did not build the projects for me, but it gave me the massive leverage required to build things that actually mattered.

## The New Baseline

Fast forward to right now, and the landscape is almost unrecognizable. We are using AI agents, Cursor, and Claude Code to outsource the manual labor of coding. 

The scariest part of looking at job listings today is realizing how much the baseline expectations have shifted to match this reality. As an aspiring software engineer entering the industry, you are no longer just expected to know how to write code. You are implicitly expected to have experience using these AI tools to write code faster and integrate intelligence into production systems. School taught me how to write a nested loop, whereas the industry expects me to know how to review and architect systems that an AI agent generated. 

I am finishing my degree in a job market where the tools I will use daily did not exist when I enrolled. The foundation my degree gave me is absolutely real, and understanding the theory is the only way to effectively manage the AI tools writing the code. But what I am walking into is unfamiliar in ways no previous batch had to navigate quite like this, and figuring out how to adapt is the real final exam.
