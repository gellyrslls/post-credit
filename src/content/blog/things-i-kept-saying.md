When I moved from Saudi Arabia to the Philippines for university in 2022, I had a version of this chapter already written in my head. This meant a new country, new people, and a hard reset on everything that felt stale about who I was. I was going to be more independent, meet people who actually shared my interests, and figure out who I was outside of the only environment I had ever known. What I did not account for was how quickly a Computer Science degree consumes the version of yourself you were planning to become. Within a few months, I had developed a phrase I would use for the next four years without ever noticing how often I was saying it: *I will do that after finals.*

I told myself that after finals I would relearn the piano, actually use that camera, and go out more. I promised I would talk to more people and stop spending every free hour either staring at a codebase or recovering from one. *After finals* became a container I kept dropping pieces of my identity into, and the container just kept getting heavier.

## The Shelved Identity

The things I kept postponing were not abstract. They were specific, and most of them traced back to something I used to genuinely be before university hollowed out the time for it.

I have a Yamaha keyboard sitting in my room that I have owned for years. Two of my closest friends play piano incredibly well rather than just casually, and every time I listened to them I had the same thought that piano is the right instrument for me, I briefly played as a kid, and I should get back to it. I never did. When semester breaks finally arrived, the exhaustion always got there first. The most I ever managed was sitting down, playing a few notes until it felt unfamiliar, and closing the lid again. This became the cycle for four years.

The video editing situation is the one that stings most specifically because I can point to the exact object that represents the gap. I spent my high school years making Premiere Pro montages and borrowing DSLRs for school projects because I did not own one. I still have edits from back then that I am genuinely proud of. When I found an old digital camera in my dad's things, I built an entire project in my head where I would romanticize my college life the way I always wanted to. The USC campus is genuinely beautiful, and my CS batchmates are genuinely interesting people. I wanted to capture the chaos of *Today's Carolinian*, the late nights, and the moments that would not mean anything to anyone who was not there, which meant I wanted to put all of that into a graduation edit I could keep. The camera stayed in my bag instead. There was always a Next.js component to fix or a thesis manuscript to revise, representing something that felt more defensible than making a memory. Productivity won every single time, and I let it.

The one thing I actually got back was basketball, which was the first sport I ever loved. Elementary school was NBA 2K, watching games, and playing in whatever court was nearby. Then high school happened and I lost it, convincing myself that I had stopped growing while everyone around me had not. The honest version is that my closest friends at the time just were not into it, so neither was I. Computer Science has a reputation for being unathletic, so when I found out that several people in my batch actually played, and that there was a group I could just show up to, something clicked back into place. Getting back on the court this past year did not just return a hobby. It gave me a sense of belonging I had been quietly missing for three years, which involved the specific feeling of sharing something physical and competitive with people you actually like. It also made the absence of the other things feel sharper by comparison.

## The Guilt of Resting

The reason all of this was so easy to postpone is that doing nothing, even briefly, carried a cost that doing something productive did not.

My first few years at USC, I was surrounded by batchmates who were building things before we had been formally taught to build anything. This involved GitHub projects, tech stacks they already knew, and internships starting in second year when the curriculum did not require one until fourth. Watching that level of output constantly did something to me that I did not fully name until much later. It was a quiet, persistent miserableness that was not directed at them, but rather ambient, creating a background feeling that whatever I was doing with my time was the wrong thing. It felt like I was supposed to be grinding too, and every hour I was not, I was falling further behind someone who was.

That pressure made me spend a lot of time in imaginary alternate timelines instead of the actual one. I thought about what it would look like to rent an apartment alone to force independence before I was ready for it. I thought about joining the CESAFI Esports League to compete and meet people from other schools. I also thought briefly about what it would be like to be in another relationship after my previous four-year relationship ended in 2022, but then I kept arriving at the same conclusion, which was that I was not emotionally or financially ready to give another person what they deserved. So I added that to the *after finals* list too.

The honest thing about all of this is that I was not wrong to feel the pressure. The gap between me and my most driven batchmates was real. What I got wrong was the conclusion, which was the belief that rest was something I had to earn rather than something that was just part of being a person.

## The Communication Gap Nobody Grades

The layer underneath all of this, which was the thing that made every social calculation harder than it needed to be, was language.

I speak Filipino and English. I suck at Cebuano, or more precisely, I can understand it reasonably well but I cannot speak it without the kind of self-consciousness that makes normal conversation feel like a performance. Moving from Saudi Arabia to Cebu at eighteen meant arriving in a place where the casual register of almost every conversation around me was in a language I could follow but not participate in naturally. That gap was invisible to most people and enormous to me.

It was not that I avoided things entirely. I went to the open-invite parties from my organization, and I tried drinking for the first time in my second year. I showed up. But showing up and actually being present in a room are two different things, and for most of my time at USC, I was doing the first one while failing at the second. I would spend so much energy mentally translating, figuring out how to phrase something in Cebuano, and actively trying not to draw attention to the fact that I was doing all of that, which meant that by the time I had worked out what to say, the conversation had moved on. I could present a technical topic in front of a classroom with no problem at all. A structured argument, a clear thesis, and questions I could anticipate were all fine. What I could not do was just be in a room and let a conversation happen without managing it.

It took until my final year to admit to myself that the language barrier had become an excuse more than an obstacle. Cebuano is a skill, and skills improve with practice. I had been avoiding the practice for three years and calling it a limitation when it was actually a fear of being caught mid-sentence not knowing the word. That realization did not fix everything immediately, but it changed the shape of the problem. It is not easy, but it is simple, and I just kept overcomplicating it.

## The Heavy In-Between

Finals are almost done now, and the structure that has organized my entire life for four years is dissolving faster than I expected.

I thought the end of university would feel like relief. What it actually feels like is a strange, quiet pressure from a different direction. The urgency of deadlines is gone, and what replaced it is not rest, but rather the awareness of everything that comes next and the fact that I do not feel fully prepared for any of it.

When I am honest about where the pressure comes from, it comes from my parents. I want to help them financially. I want to eventually retire them, which means being in a position where I can give back to them the way they gave to me and my siblings for our entire lives. That goal is not abstract. It is specific, heavy, and it sits underneath almost every career decision I have made or am about to make. It makes taking things day by day feel like advice written for someone else's life.

What I am trying to figure out right now in this strange in-between period is how to hold all of that without letting it collapse the smaller things. The camera is still in my bag, and I have not made the graduation edit yet. The Yamaha keyboard is still waiting. These are not urgent, but they are real, and I think part of what this post is actually about is giving myself permission to treat them as real. This means viewing them not as rewards I have to earn by sorting out the big things first, but as things that are just part of a life I am allowed to be living right now.

The work mattered, and the late nights were not wasted. But the version of myself that I kept promising to return to after finals deserves more than a task slot on a to-do list. I am just trying to practice taking him seriously, even before everything else is resolved.
